National Income Accounting

Output Concepts

Domestic product All goods and services produced within national borders
National product All goods and services produced by national residents, even if production takes place abroad.
Gross Product Includes replacement of depreciated capital
Net Product Gross Product - Depreciation

Income Concepts

Disposable income [Labor income + capital income + transfers] to persons,
net of taxes and social insurance contributions

Investment Concepts

Investment = [Business fixed investment] + [Residential investment] + [Inventory investment]

Business fixed investment  =  [Inv. in plant and equipment]  +  [Inv. in structures]

Net investment Change in the capital stock
Gross investment Net investment  +  Replacement investment
   

Savings Concepts

Personal saving

[Disposable income] – [Personal outlays]
Personal outlays are mostly consumption and interest payments.
National saving Private + public saving
Private saving Saving by persons and corporations
Public saving Minus the public sector budget deficit
Gross/net saving Gross saving includes replacement of depreciation. Net saving does not.

 

Macro Concepts

ECI:  Employment cost index. Measures total compensation paid to workers, including both wages and salaries and the cost of benefits such as health plans.

Labor force: All employed and unemployed persons. The labor force participation rate is the ratio of the labor force to the working age population.

NAIRU:  "Non-accelerating inflation rate of unemployment." The unemployment rate that is consistent with constant inflation.

Okun's law: A negative relationship between the change in unemployment and growth. Allows ot estimate the GDP growth rate that is sustainable in the long-run.

Phillips curve:  A negative relationship between inflation and unemployment. Many versions exist. There is wide agreement that the long-run Phillips curve is vertical: Additional inflation does not buy higher employment.

Recession: A downturn in the business cycle. There are many operational definitions such as "2 consecutive quarters with negative real GDP growth." The official dating is done by an NBER committee. It does not follow strict rules, but uses judgement.

Unemployment:  Persons are categorized as employed, unemployed, or not in the labor force. A person counts as unemployed, if he/she is not currently employed, but looking for a job or waiting for a job to start. All other non-employed are not considered part of the labor force. This includes those who have given up the job search process (the discouraged).

Micro Concepts

Moral hazard:  Agents engage in excessive risk-taking because they are covered by some form of insurance.

Statistical Terminology

Mean: Average value of a random variable

Normal distribution:  (Gaussian distribution)  The well-known bell-shaped distribution. Many "natural" processes generate normally distributed random variables. For example: Toss a coin many times and count the fraction coming up "heads." This is normally distributed.

Standard deviation: Average size of the fluctuation of a random variable. Square root of the variance. Example: The standard deviation of business cycle peaks for GDP is 1.7%. This means that on average GDP is 1.7% above its trend level at the end of a boom.

Trend:  Draw a smooth line through a time series and you get its trend. Many formal methods exist for "drawing" the trend.

Variance:  Var(x) = E( [x - mean(x)]^2 )
